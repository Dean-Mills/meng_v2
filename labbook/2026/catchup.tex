\subsection{Related Work}

This section summarises the existing work that my contributions build on, compete with, or were inspired by. I organise it by problem area rather than chronologically.

\subsubsection{Bottom-up multi-person pose estimation}

Bottom-up methods detect all keypoints in an image first, then group them into individual people. The grouping step is the core problem this thesis addresses.

\textbf{Part Affinity Fields (PAFs).} OpenPose~\cite{cao2017openpose} introduced 2D vector fields that encode limb direction and association strength between pairs of joint types. Grouping is a greedy bipartite matching over the PAF scores. This was the first real-time bottom-up method and remains the most widely known baseline, but the handcrafted matching heuristic limits its accuracy on crowded scenes.

\textbf{Associative Embeddings (AE).} Newell and Deng~\cite{newell2017associative} proposed learning a per-keypoint tag embedding alongside the heatmap detector. Keypoints with similar tags are grouped together via a simple greedy algorithm. HigherHRNet~\cite{cheng2020higherhrnet} extended this with a high-resolution backbone~\cite{sun2019hrnet} and multi-scale heatmap aggregation, achieving strong results on COCO. The AE paradigm is the closest existing work to my contrastive embedding approach --- both learn per-joint embeddings and cluster them. The key difference is that AE is trained end-to-end with the detector on real data, while my SA-GAT operates on a separately constructed graph and is trained on synthetic data with a contrastive loss.

\textbf{Direct regression.} DEKR~\cite{geng2021dekr} bypasses explicit grouping entirely by regressing per-pixel offset vectors from each keypoint to its person centre. The offsets implicitly define the grouping. This is a fundamentally different paradigm --- there is no embedding space and no clustering step.

\textbf{End-to-end transformers.} ED-Pose~\cite{yang2023edpose} and GroupPose~\cite{liu2023grouppose} treat multi-person pose as a set prediction problem using DETR-style architectures. Each query directly predicts a full person skeleton, so detection and grouping are unified in a single transformer decoder. These are the most modern methods (ICLR 2023, ICCV 2023) and achieve state-of-the-art results, but require large-scale training and are architecturally very different from the graph-based approach I investigate.

\subsubsection{GNN-based pose grouping}

A small but growing body of work applies graph neural networks specifically to the keypoint grouping problem.

\textbf{Hierarchical Graph Grouping (HGG).} Jin et al.~\cite{jin2020hgg} cast multi-person pose estimation as a graph clustering problem with keypoints as nodes. They use edge discriminators and macro-node discriminators to hierarchically merge nodes into person clusters. This is the closest prior work to my overall approach --- both use GNNs on keypoint graphs for grouping. The key differences are: (1) HGG uses a hierarchical merging strategy while I use a flat clustering/OT assignment, (2) HGG is trained end-to-end with the detector while my method operates on a separate embedding stage, and (3) HGG does not incorporate skeleton-aware structural priors.

\textbf{CenterGroup.} Bras\'o et al.~\cite{braso2021centergroup} use a transformer to produce context-aware embeddings for detected keypoints and person centres, then apply multi-head cross-attention to directly group joints to their corresponding centres. This is a learned grouping mechanism trained end-to-end, achieving state-of-the-art speed and accuracy. My SA-GAT's type-pair attention bias is conceptually related to CenterGroup's use of attention for grouping, though the architectures differ substantially.

\textbf{Instance-aware attention.} Yang et al.~\cite{yang2023instanceaware} supervise the self-attention weights in a vision transformer to be instance-aware, so that keypoints belonging to the same person attend to each other. The attention matrix directly provides the grouping. This work demonstrates that attention mechanisms can encode grouping structure, which motivates my SA-GAT's repulsion heads that explicitly separate same-type keypoints.

\textbf{Graph Edge Classification (GEC).} Maher et al.~\cite{maher2025gec} published a binary edge classification approach for keypoint grouping in early 2025, using edge features to predict same-person / different-person edges. This is essentially the same idea as my abandoned skeleton-aware edge classifier (Option A), confirming both the viability of the approach and that it had been independently explored.

\subsubsection{Graph clustering with GNNs}

Several general-purpose GNN clustering methods informed my SA-DMoN experiments.

\textbf{DMoN.} Tsitsulin et al.~\cite{tsitsulin2023dmon} proposed Deep Modularity Networks, which learn soft cluster assignments by maximising a differentiable relaxation of the modularity objective from spectral graph theory~\cite{vonluxburg2007spectral}. DMoN was the starting point for my SA-DMoN contribution. My experiments revealed that the modularity objective is fundamentally misaligned with pose grouping: the null model encodes spatial proximity, but communities are defined by identity. No null model modification fixed this (see Experiments 4--10).

\textbf{DiffPool.} Ying et al.~\cite{ying2018diffpool} introduced differentiable soft assignment with a link prediction auxiliary loss for hierarchical graph coarsening. DiffPool's soft assignment mechanism influenced my thinking about assignment matrices, though I ultimately used Sinkhorn OT rather than learned pooling.

\textbf{DC-GNN.} Dong and Klabjan~\cite{dong2024dcgnn} proposed optimal transport-based clustering with Sinkhorn iterations on a bipartite graph of nodes and cluster-nodes. This is the closest general-purpose GNN clustering method to my SCOT approach. The key difference is that SCOT uses typed skeleton slots (each slot expects a specific joint type) rather than generic cluster-nodes, and applies hard type constraints via infinite cost masking.

\subsubsection{Face clustering with GNNs}

GNN-based clustering has been more extensively studied in face recognition, and several ideas transferred to my work.

Yang et al.~\cite{yang2019face} proposed a GCN with edge detection and segmentation modules for face clustering on an affinity graph, establishing that GNNs can effectively predict pairwise same-identity / different-identity relationships. Follow-up work (GCN-V/E)~\cite{yang2020gcnve} separated vertex confidence estimation from edge connectivity prediction. These edge classification approaches inspired my initial skeleton-aware edge classifier direction (Option A), which was later abandoned in favour of SCOT after the GEC paper~\cite{maher2025gec} demonstrated independent parallel work on the same idea.

\subsubsection{Optimal transport in vision}

Optimal transport has been applied to several vision problems, but not to skeleton-constrained keypoint grouping prior to SCOT.

\textbf{Sinkhorn algorithm.} Cuturi~\cite{cuturi2013sinkhorn} introduced entropy-regularised optimal transport with the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm, making OT computationally tractable for machine learning. SCOT uses Sinkhorn iterations in log-domain for numerical stability.

\textbf{OTA.} Ge et al.~\cite{ge2021ota} applied OT to label assignment in object detection, using Sinkhorn to optimally match anchors to ground truth boxes. This uses flat, untyped targets --- there is no skeleton structure or type constraint.

\textbf{KPG-RL.} Gu et al.~\cite{gu2022kpgrl} used keypoint-guided OT for heterogeneous domain adaptation, where keypoints guide the transport plan between source and target distributions. Despite the name overlap, this is a completely different problem --- the ``keypoints'' are landmark correspondences for domain alignment, not body joint detections.

\textbf{Slot attention and OT.} Chang et al.~\cite{chang2022slotot} connected slot attention to Sinkhorn OT for object discovery, showing that slot attention's competitive assignment can be interpreted as an OT problem. SCOT's typed slot structure is a more constrained version of this idea, with hard type masks that enforce skeleton semantics.

None of these prior OT methods combine typed slots, skeleton structure, and hard joint-type constraints. This is the novelty that SCOT contributes.

\subsubsection{Constrained clustering}

\textbf{COP-Kmeans.} Wagstaff et al.~\cite{wagstaff2001copkmeans} proposed constrained k-means with must-link and cannot-link constraints. My COP-Kmeans adaptation uses cannot-link constraints between same-type keypoints --- two left elbows cannot belong to the same person. This is a simple but powerful structural prior that requires no learned components beyond the embedding space.

The old code had separate files for everything --- \texttt{virtual\_dataloader.py}, \texttt{virtual\_dataset.py}, \texttt{virtual\_preprocessor.py} as well as \texttt{dataloader.py}, \texttt{dataset.py}, \texttt{preprocessor.py}, \texttt{depth\_estimation.py}. The architecture has been updated to use a unified pipeline:

\begin{verbatim}
COCO data          Virtual data
    |                   |
coco_adapter.py    virtual_adapter.py
         |         |
        dataset.py          <-- single Dataset class
             |

             |
        dataloader.py       <-- single DataLoader
\end{verbatim}

Two reasons for this:
\begin{compactitem}
  \item After the adapter it doesn't matter where the data came from, the rest of the pipeline is identical
  \item Can train on synthetic data first then swap to COCO without changing anything downstream
\end{compactitem}


\subsubsection{Extractor changes}

Keypoint format changed from $[x,y,v]$ to $[x,y,z,v]$ per joint (51 $\to$ 68 values per person). Per-joint depth is strictly better than one depth value per person. $z$ is world-space Euclidean distance from camera to joint in metres --- consistent across all camera positions, matches MiDaS~\cite{ranftl2020midas} semantics after normalisation.

Visibility changed from $0/2$ to $0/1/2$ with raycast occlusion detection. Occluded joints are real training signal --- marking them as invisible was losing information.

\texttt{calculate\_distance\_to\_camera} was removed --- redundant now that there is per-joint $z$.

Out-of-frame joints position changed from $[0.0, 0.0]$ to $[-1.0, -1.0]$. Zero is a real pixel location (top-left corner), $-1$ is an unambiguous sentinel meaning ``no valid position''. The GAT uses visibility $v=0$ to ignore these joints during message passing but the position itself should also signal invalidity explicitly.


\subsubsection{Renderer changes}

Filenames changed to \texttt{<guid>\_<n>.png} / \texttt{<guid>\_<n>.json}. GUIDs eliminate ID collisions, the \texttt{\_n} suffix allows filtering by person count without parsing JSON. \texttt{num\_people} was added to image metadata for debugging and dataset statistics.


\subsubsection{Virtual adapter}

Loads a folder of \texttt{<guid>\_n.png/<guid>\_n.json} pairs and scans the folder to filter using min and max people. Converts from the per-image JSON (68 floats per person) to the unified output:

\begin{verbatim}
{
    "image":      tensor [3, H, W],
    "keypoints":  list of tensor [17, 4],  # [x, y, z, v]
    "img_id":     str,
    "num_people": int
}
\end{verbatim}

No transforms, no normalisation, no MiDaS --- just reading and reshaping. Everything else happens downstream.


\subsubsection{COCO adapter}

Loads COCO 2017~\cite{lin2014coco} person keypoint annotations via pycocotools, filters by min/max person count the same way as the virtual adapter. MiDaS DPT\_Hybrid is initialised once at construction time and used to estimate a depth map per image. Depth is then sampled at each keypoint's pixel location.

Converts from COCO's flat format (51 floats per person, COCO visibility 0/1/2) to the same unified output as the virtual adapter.

Visibility is handled explicitly:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
COCO $v$ & Position & Depth \\
\midrule
0 (not labelled) & $[-1, -1]$ & 0 \\
1 (occluded) & real $x, y$ & MiDaS sampled \\
2 (visible) & real $x, y$ & MiDaS sampled \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}


\subsubsection{Dataset}

Single shared Dataset class that sits between the adapters and the rest of the pipeline. Accepts either adapter and applies a consistent transform so everything downstream sees the same tensor shapes regardless of data source.

\begin{compactitem}
  \item Image resized to fit within \texttt{target\_size} $\times$ \texttt{target\_size} preserving aspect ratio
  \item Symmetrically padded to exactly \texttt{target\_size} $\times$ \texttt{target\_size}
  \item Keypoint $x, y$ scaled and shifted to match the new image dimensions
  \item $z$ and $v$ columns left untouched
  \item Joints with $v=0$ (sentinel $-1, -1$) are not transformed
\end{compactitem}


\subsubsection{Dataloader}

Wraps a PoseDataset with a custom collate function. Images stacked into a single tensor, keypoints kept as a list of lists since person count varies per image. \texttt{batch\_size} is an explicit argument controlled from \texttt{config.yaml}.


\subsubsection{Preprocessor}

Single unified graph builder. Converts a DataLoader batch into PyTorch Geometric graphs ready for the GAT. One graph per image, all people's joints are nodes in the same graph.

Joints with $v=0$ are excluded entirely --- no position, no node. Everything else becomes a node with features:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Feature & Description \\
\midrule
$x_{\text{norm}}$ & $x$ normalised to $[0, 1]$ by image size \\
$y_{\text{norm}}$ & $y$ normalised to $[0, 1]$ by image size \\
$z_{\text{norm}}$ & depth normalised per-graph min-max over valid joints \\
$v_{\text{norm}}$ & visibility normalised ($0 \to 0.0$, $1 \to 0.5$, $2 \to 1.0$) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Separate tensors per graph: \texttt{joint\_types} ($[N]$ long, index 0--16 for GAT embedding layer) and \texttt{person\_labels} ($[N]$ long, ground truth person assignment for training).

Edges are built using kNN with $k=8$ on $(x_{\text{norm}}, y_{\text{norm}}, z_{\text{norm}})$ --- depth is part of the proximity calculation so joints that are close in 3D get connected even if far apart in 2D.

Key decisions:
\begin{compactitem}
  \item Per-graph depth normalisation --- MiDaS and metric depth have different absolute scales, normalising per-graph makes both sources comparable
  \item \texttt{joint\_types} separate from features --- lets the GAT learn a per-joint-type embedding independently of position
  \item kNN includes depth --- richer connectivity than 2D-only
\end{compactitem}

All pipeline tests passed --- see \texttt{outputs/test\_outputs/data}.

\subsection{GAT}

The GAT (Graph Attention Network) takes the graph of joints built by the preprocessor and produces a 128-dimensional embedding for each joint. The goal is for joints belonging to the same person to end up close together in this 128D space, and joints from different people to be far apart.

The architecture:
\begin{compactitem}
  \item Each joint starts as a 4-value feature vector $[x, y, z, v]$ plus a learned 32-dim embedding for its joint type (nose, left knee, etc.)
  \item Two GATv2~\cite{brody2022gatv2} layers with 4 attention heads do message passing --- each joint updates its representation by attending to its neighbours
  \item A final linear projection maps down to 128 dimensions
  \item L2 normalisation so all embeddings live on the unit hypersphere
\end{compactitem}

The joint type embedding matters --- a left knee should have different characteristics than a nose regardless of position, and keeping this separate from the spatial features lets the GAT learn that independently.


\subsubsection{Training (isolation phase)}

The GAT is trained in isolation using contrastive loss~\cite{chopra2005contrastive} only. Pull same-person joint embeddings together, push different-person joint embeddings apart. No grouping head yet --- just verifying the embedding space is structured correctly before adding the next component.

Config:
\begin{verbatim}
gat:
  num_joint_types: 17
  joint_embedding_dim: 32
  raw_feature_dim: 4
  hidden_dim: 64
  output_dim: 128
  num_layers: 2
  num_heads: 4
  dropout: 0.1
  use_layer_norm: true
  l2_normalize: true

loss:
  contrastive_margin: 0.5
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{DEC (Deep Embedded Clustering)}

DEC takes embeddings and learns $K$ cluster centres in the same embedding space. Each joint gets a soft assignment $q$ based on distance to each centre using a Student's $t$-distribution. A sharpened target distribution $p$ is computed from $q$ and the model is trained to minimise KL divergence between them --- pushing soft assignments toward harder, more confident ones over time.

Cluster centres are initialised with k-means++ before training. One important detail: $p$ is updated every \texttt{update\_interval} steps rather than every step. Updating every step causes cluster collapse --- the model chases a moving target and all joints pile into one cluster by step 150. Periodic updates let the centres stabilise between target refreshes.


\subsubsection{What I tried and why it kept failing}

\textbf{Attempt 1 --- random GAT embeddings.}
With an untrained GAT all joints are roughly equidistant from all cluster centres, so $q$ is nearly uniform across all $K$ clusters. $p$ is computed from $q$ so $p \approx q$, which means $\text{KL}(p \| q) \approx 0$ immediately --- no loss, no gradient, no learning.

\textbf{Attempt 2 --- pre-trained GAT embeddings.}
With 200 steps of GAT pre-training, k-means++ initialised perfectly (accuracy 1.0 before any DEC training). But then DEC actively degraded:

\begin{verbatim}
Before training: accuracy = 1.0000
Step  25: accuracy=1.0000  loss=0.0661
...
Step 125: accuracy=0.5765  loss=0.0005
Step 150: accuracy=0.2000  loss=0.0002
\end{verbatim}

This is cluster collapse --- $p$ was being recomputed from $q$ every step, the model chased a moving target, and all joints piled into one cluster. Fixed with periodic $p$ updates but a deeper problem remained: if the GAT is good there's nothing for DEC to do.

\textbf{Attempt 3 --- periodic updates, real scale (17 joints per person, 128D).}
Switched to fabricated embeddings (Gaussian blobs on the unit hypersphere with known ground truth) to control difficulty. Hit a hard ceiling:

\begin{verbatim}
Step 250: accuracy=0.7647  loss=0.0273
Step 300: accuracy=0.7647  loss=0.0272
...
Step 1000: accuracy=0.7647  loss=0.0274
\end{verbatim}

0.7647 exactly --- 4 out of 5 clusters correct, one pair always confused regardless of noise level or number of steps. This is the curse of dimensionality --- 17 points in 128D is too sparse for the Student's $t$ soft assignments to produce a meaningful gradient.

\textbf{Attempt 4 --- 50 joints per person.}
Bumped \texttt{n\_per\_person} to 50:

\begin{verbatim}
Before training: accuracy = 0.6200
Step 250: accuracy=0.9680
Step 300: accuracy=1.0000
\end{verbatim}

DEC works --- but only with 3$\times$ more points per cluster than the real problem has.

\subsubsection{Why DEC isn't the right head}

DEC is fully unsupervised --- it never sees ground truth labels. It can only sharpen assignments that are already roughly correct, it has no signal to fix wrong ones. With 17 joints per person in 128D there is a hard ceiling at 0.8 regardless of tuning. If the GAT is doing its job, kNN accuracy on the embeddings is already 0.98--1.0 and k-means++ alone at inference time would give near-perfect assignments. DEC adds complexity and collapse risk without improving on the initialisation.

The right grouping head needs to learn from ground truth labels. Graph partitioning and slot attention both do this.

\subsection{Slot Attention}

\subsubsection{Why slot attention}

DEC failed at realistic scale because it is unsupervised. Slot attention is trained end-to-end with ground truth person assignments via Hungarian-matched cross entropy.

\subsubsection{What it does}

Slot attention is an iterative competitive attention mechanism. $K$ slot vectors are initialised by sampling from a learned Gaussian distribution, one slot per person. Over several iterations each slot attends to all joints and competes for the ones it best represents. The competition is enforced by applying softmax over slots rather than over joints --- each joint's attention weights sum to 1 across all slots, so if two slots compete for the same joint the better-matching one wins.

After iterative refinement the final slot vectors score each joint via dot product, producing logits $[N, K]$. Hungarian matching finds the optimal bijection between predicted slots and ground truth people, and cross entropy is computed on the matched assignments.

The architecture:
\begin{verbatim}
Inputs:  embeddings [N, D]  L2-normalised GAT embeddings
         k          int     number of people in this scene

Slots sampled from learned mu, sigma  ->  [K, D]

For num_iterations:
    norm(slots)   ->  queries  [K, D]
    norm(inputs)  ->  keys     [N, D]
                      values   [N, D]

    scores   =  queries . keys^T * scale     [K, N]
    softmax over slots  (competition)        [K, N]
    normalise within each slot               [K, N]
    updates  =  weighted sum of values       [K, D]
    slots    =  GRU(updates, slots)          [K, D]
    slots    =  slots + FF(LayerNorm(slots)) [K, D]

slot_keys  =  linear(slots)                 [K, D]
logits     =  embeddings . slot_keys^T      [N, K]
\end{verbatim}

Loss: Hungarian matching between predicted slots and ground truth people, then cross entropy on matched assignments.

Config:
\begin{verbatim}
slot_attention:
  num_iterations: 7
  slot_weight:    1.0
\end{verbatim}


\subsubsection{What went wrong during testing}

\textbf{Instability at lr=$10^{-3}$, 3 iterations} --- the model found the correct solution early (accuracy 1.0 at step 100) but then overshot and lost it, ending at 0.78. Slots reinitialise from the learned Gaussian every forward pass --- with only 3 iterations they do not always converge, producing noisy gradient signal.

\textbf{Fix 1 --- more iterations.} Increasing \texttt{num\_iterations} from 3 to 7 stabilised the assignments within each forward pass and produced cleaner gradient signal.

\textbf{Fix 2 --- cosine LR schedule.} A flat lr of $10^{-3}$ caused overshooting. A cosine schedule decaying from $10^{-3}$ to $10^{-5}$ allows fast early learning and fine-grained settling.

\subsection{Graph Partitioning}

\subsubsection{What it does}

Graph partitioning frames the grouping problem as binary edge classification. For every pair of joints $(i, j)$ the model predicts whether they belong to the same person. After thresholding, connected components on the resulting affinity graph recover the person groups without any explicit knowledge of $K$.

This is a fundamentally different framing from slot attention. Slot attention assigns joints to $K$ pre-specified slots via competitive attention. Graph partitioning makes pairwise same/different decisions and lets the group structure emerge from the graph topology. $K$ does not need to be specified at inference time --- it falls out naturally from the number of connected components.

For each pair $(i, j)$ the feature vector is:
\begin{equation}
f(i, j) = [e_i - e_j \; \| \; e_i \odot e_j] \in \mathbb{R}^{2D}
\end{equation}
where $e_i, e_j$ are L2-normalised GAT embeddings. The difference captures direction between the two points in embedding space; the element-wise product captures co-activation. The MLP maps $\mathbb{R}^{2D}$ to a scalar logit per pair.

Groups are recovered at inference by thresholding predicted affinities at 0.5 and finding connected components via union-find.

Loss is binary cross entropy over all $N(N-1)/2$ pairs. With 5 people $\times$ 17 joints there are 680 same-person pairs and 2890 different-person pairs --- a 4:1 class imbalance. \texttt{pos\_weight = n\_neg / n\_pos} (clamped at 10) upscales the positive class.

Two metrics tracked:
\begin{compactitem}
  \item \textbf{Edge F1} --- precision/recall balance on the binary pair classification (accuracy is misleading under class imbalance)
  \item \textbf{Grouping accuracy} --- end-to-end metric: thresholded edges $\to$ connected components $\to$ Hungarian matching against ground truth
\end{compactitem}

\subsection{DETR --- Rationale for Exclusion}

DETR was considered as a fourth grouping head but not pursued to isolation testing.

\textbf{Architectural overlap with slot attention.} DETR's decoder is a superset of slot attention --- it adds self-attention between queries and a heavier feed-forward stack, but the core mechanism is the same iterative cross-attention competition. The additional complexity adds no meaningful capability for this problem.

\textbf{The null object problem.} DETR handles variable detection counts by padding predictions with a learned ``no object'' class. Resolving the null value instability encountered in the preliminary implementation requires careful loss masking and balanced sampling --- engineering overhead with no benefit for this problem where the number of people per scene is bounded and known at training time.

\textbf{The comparison is already complete without it.} The four approaches evaluated (kNN on GAT embeddings, DEC, slot attention, graph partitioning) span the relevant design space: unsupervised vs supervised, slot-based vs pairwise, $K$-required vs $K$-free. DETR would not add a meaningfully distinct data point.

\subsection{Experimental Results}

\subsubsection{Metrics}

\textbf{PGA (Pose Grouping Accuracy).} The primary metric. For each scene, predicted person groups are matched to ground truth people via Hungarian matching --- finding the optimal bijection between predicted and ground truth labels. PGA is the fraction of joints assigned to the correct person under this optimal matching. A score of 1.0 means every joint was grouped correctly. This metric is permutation-invariant.

\textbf{NMI (Normalised Mutual Information).} Measures the quality of the GAT embeddings independently of the grouping head. K-means is run on the embeddings with ground truth $K$, and NMI measures how much information the resulting clusters share with the ground truth person labels. NMI is the same across all methods evaluated from the same checkpoint because it depends only on the embeddings, not the grouping head.

\textbf{ARI (Adjusted Rand Index).} A second embedding quality metric. Measures agreement between k-means clustering and ground truth, corrected for chance. Like NMI, it is the same across all heads from the same checkpoint.

\textbf{Detection F1.} Measures whether the method correctly counts the number of people in the scene. For kNN this is always 1.0 because ground truth $K$ is provided. For slot attention $K$ is also provided. For graph partitioning $K$ is inferred from connected components --- if the edge threshold splits or merges groups, F1 drops.

\textbf{Per-joint accuracy.} PGA broken down by COCO joint type. Reveals whether certain joints are harder to group correctly --- typically extremities (ankles, wrists) which are more likely to be occluded or close to another person's joints.

\subsection{SA-DMoN (Skeleton-Aware Deep Modularity Networks)}

\subsubsection{What DMoN is}

DMoN (Deep Modularity Networks) is a differentiable graph clustering method from Tsitsulin et al.~\cite{tsitsulin2023dmon}. It learns to partition a graph into communities by maximising a differentiable relaxation of the modularity objective from spectral graph theory~\cite{vonluxburg2007spectral}.

Given a graph with adjacency matrix $A$, find a soft assignment matrix $S \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$ where $S_{ik}$ is the probability that node $i$ belongs to cluster $k$. DMoN optimises three losses simultaneously:

\textbf{Spectral modularity loss.} The modularity matrix is:
\begin{equation}
B = A - \frac{dd^\top}{2m}
\end{equation}
where $d$ is the degree vector and $m = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i d_i$ is the total edge mass. The spectral loss is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{spectral}} = -\frac{\text{Tr}(S^\top B S)}{2m}
\end{equation}
Minimising this maximises the trace of $S^\top B S$, meaning nodes in the same cluster are more connected than expected under the null model.

\textbf{Orthogonality regularisation.} Encourages cluster assignments to be quasi-orthogonal:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{ortho}} = \left\| \frac{S^\top S}{\|S^\top S\|_F} - \frac{I_K}{\sqrt{K}} \right\|_F
\end{equation}

\textbf{Collapse prevention.} Prevents the trivial solution of assigning all nodes to a single cluster:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{cluster}} = \frac{\sqrt{K}}{N} \left\| \sum_i S_i \right\|_F - 1
\end{equation}


\subsubsection{Why DMoN for this problem}

The previous experiments established that the GAT produces good embeddings (kNN accuracy 0.98+ on synthetic data), the sim-to-real gap (0.994 $\to$ 0.838 PGA) is the primary bottleneck, and supervised heads didn't improve the GAT embeddings.

The hypothesis behind DMoN is that the modularity loss provides a \textbf{domain-invariant structural prior}. Unlike contrastive loss or supervised CE which depend on learned visual features, modularity reasons about graph topology --- are these nodes more connected to each other than expected? This topological signal should persist even when visual features degrade on out-of-distribution data.

Vanilla DMoN is fully unsupervised, which would put it in the same category as DEC. The contribution here is three domain-specific modifications.


\subsubsection{Modification 1: Keypoint-type exclusivity regularisation}

In standard graph clustering any node can go in any cluster. But in pose grouping each person has at most one keypoint of each type. DMoN has no mechanism to enforce this.

Define a type indicator matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times T}$ where $M_{it} = 1$ if keypoint $i$ is of type $t$ (with $T = 17$ for COCO joints). Then $M^\top S \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times K}$ gives, for each cluster $k$ and type $t$, the total soft assignment mass. The type exclusivity loss is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{type}} = \| \text{ReLU}(M^\top S - 1) \|_F^2
\end{equation}
This penalises any cluster that accumulates more than one keypoint of the same type. It is fully differentiable and directly encodes a structural constraint of the problem that no general-purpose graph clustering method captures.


\subsubsection{Modification 2: Supervised-modularity hybrid loss}

Rather than discarding the modularity objective in favour of pure supervision, the approach combines both:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \lambda_1 \cdot \text{CE}(S, S_{\text{gt}}) + \lambda_2 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{spectral}} + \lambda_3 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{cluster}} + \lambda_4 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{type}} + \lambda_5 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{ortho}}
\end{equation}
where $\text{CE}(S, S_{\text{gt}})$ is Hungarian-matched cross entropy. The supervised CE handles the ``what to group'' signal during training. The modularity loss handles the ``how to group structurally'' signal that should transfer across domains.


\subsubsection{Implementation}

Standard DMoN uses a fixed-$K$ MLP for assignments. This doesn't work for this pipeline where $K$ varies per scene (2--5 people). The implementation uses a pool of $K_{\text{max}}$ learned cluster centre vectors. For a scene with $K$ people, the first $K$ centres are selected. Soft assignments are computed via scaled dot product:
\begin{equation}
S = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{h \cdot C_{:K}^\top}{\sqrt{H}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $h = \text{MLP}(e)$ maps GAT embeddings to a hidden space and $C \in \mathbb{R}^{K_{\text{max}} \times H}$ is the learned centre pool. Hungarian matching in the loss makes this permutation-invariant.

Config:
\begin{verbatim}
dmon:
  hidden_dim: 256
  k_max: 10
  dropout: 0.0
  lambda_spectral: 0.1
  lambda_ortho: 0.1
  lambda_cluster: 0.1
  lambda_type: 1.0
  lambda_supervised: 1.0
\end{verbatim}

The structural losses (spectral, ortho, cluster) are weighted at 0.1 relative to supervised CE and type exclusivity at 1.0. This ensures the supervised signal dominates early learning while the structural terms act as regularisers. With equal weights the ortho loss ($\sim$1.05) dominated the total loss and drowned out the CE gradient, preventing convergence.


\subsubsection{Design decisions}

\textbf{Stochastic vs deterministic centres.} The first implementation sampled cluster centres from a learned Gaussian each forward pass (same approach as slot attention). This caused severe instability --- accuracy oscillated wildly between 0.3 and 0.7 because the same input produced different assignments on different forward passes. Slot attention overcomes this with 7 iterations of competitive refinement; without that iterative convergence, the stochasticity kills learning. The fix was switching to deterministic learned centre vectors from a fixed pool.

\textbf{Loss weight balance.} With all lambda values at 1.0, the ortho loss ($\sim$1.05) was roughly equal to the CE loss ($\sim$1.6) and neither moved meaningfully. Setting structural loss weights to 0.1 let CE dominate while keeping the modularity signal as a regulariser.

\subsection{Removing Depth --- Isolating the Grouping Problem}

\subsubsection{Why remove depth}

The previous experiments showed kNN at 0.994 PGA on synthetic data. That's near-perfect --- the problem was too easy. Depth (per-joint $z$ from ground truth camera distance) was doing most of the work separating people in 3D space. With depth the GAT barely needs to learn anything beyond spatial proximity, and a grouping head has no room to add value over simple k-means.

This is also why DMoN's spectral loss was ineffective --- it's trying to improve on an already-solved problem.

Removing depth creates a harder, more realistic baseline. On COCO the depth signal comes from MiDaS estimation (noisy, arbitrary scale) rather than ground truth metric distance, so the with-depth synthetic results were artificially inflated. Training without depth forces the GAT and grouping head to work with 2D position and visibility only --- the same signal quality available on real images without a depth estimator.

A \texttt{use\_depth} flag was added to \texttt{GATConfig} and threaded through the preprocessor, trainer, and evaluator. When \texttt{use\_depth: false}: node features become $[x_{\text{norm}}, y_{\text{norm}}, v_{\text{norm}}]$ (3 values not 4), kNN edges are built on $[x_{\text{norm}}, y_{\text{norm}}]$ only, and the GAT \texttt{input\_dim} is computed accordingly.

\textbf{2. DMoN does not beat kNN --- the vanilla spectral loss hurts.} The DMoN head (0.785) is worse than kNN on the same embeddings (0.881), and kNN on the DMoN-trained GAT (0.881) is worse than kNN on the contrastive-only GAT (0.901). The vanilla spectral loss degrades performance in two ways: the head itself groups worse than k-means, and backprop through the spectral loss pushes the GAT toward embeddings that satisfy the wrong clustering criterion.

\textbf{3. The degree-based null model is the root cause.} The modularity matrix $B = A - dd^\top / (2m)$ asks ``are these keypoints more connected than their degree would predict?'' For a kNN graph built on 2D position this is the wrong question. Two shoulders from different people that happen to be spatially close will be kNN neighbours --- the degree null treats this as evidence for same-cluster. Meanwhile a left wrist and left ankle from the same person that are far apart in 2D get little credit for being grouped together.

\textbf{4. This directly motivates the skeleton-aware null model.} Instead of asking ``connected more than degree predicts,'' the skeleton-aware null asks ``connected more than spatial proximity and anatomical adjacency predict.'' Two nearby shoulders from different people become \emph{expected} under the null rather than surprising.


% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{SA-DMoN v1 --- Skeleton-Aware Null Model}

A new \texttt{sa\_dmon.py} module was created alongside the existing \texttt{dmon.py}. The architecture is identical to vanilla DMoN --- the only change is in the spectral loss, which replaces the degree-based null with the skeleton-aware null model.

The skeleton-aware null combines two terms:

\textbf{Spatial proximity kernel $P$.} For each pair of nodes:
\begin{equation}
P_{i,j} = \exp\left(-\frac{\|pos_i - pos_j\|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
\end{equation}
where $pos_i$ is the normalised 2D position $(x, y) \in [0, 1]^2$. Sigma is learnable, stored as $\log(\sigma)$ for unconstrained optimisation, initialised at 0.2.

\textbf{Anatomical type affinity $T$.} A fixed $17 \times 17$ matrix derived from the COCO skeleton topology via BFS: directly connected joints get 1.0 (e.g.\ left\_shoulder $\leftrightarrow$ left\_elbow), two hops get 0.5, three hops get 0.25, further or unconnected get 0.0.

\textbf{Combined null $R$.} For keypoints $i$ (type $a$) and $j$ (type $b$):
\begin{equation}
R_{i,j} = T_{a,b} \cdot P_{i,j}
\end{equation}
Normalised to match the total edge mass: $R_{\text{norm}} = R \cdot (2m / \sum R)$.

\textbf{Modified modularity matrix:}
\begin{equation}
B_{\text{pose}} = A - R_{\text{norm}}
\end{equation}
The spectral loss maximises $\text{Tr}(S^\top B_{\text{pose}} S) / (2m)$, rewarding grouping joints that are connected beyond what spatial proximity and anatomical adjacency would explain.

\subsection{SA-DMoN v2 --- Diagnosing and Fixing Signal Cancellation}

\subsubsection{The diagnosis}

Looking back at the four sigma experiments, the problem is more specific than ``modularity doesn't work for pose graphs.'' The problem is \textbf{signal cancellation between the adjacency and the null model}.

The adjacency matrix $A$ is built from kNN on 2D spatial positions --- it encodes ``these keypoints are spatially close.'' The skeleton-aware null model $R_{\text{norm}}$ also encodes ``these keypoints are spatially close'' (via the Gaussian kernel on 2D positions, modulated by anatomical affinity). When computing the modularity matrix:
\[
B_{\text{pose}} = A - R_{\text{norm}}
\]
the result is subtracting a spatial signal from a spatial signal. $B_{\text{pose}}$ ends up as near-zero noise everywhere because $A$ and $R$ are highly correlated. There is nothing left for modularity to optimise. The degree-based null in vanilla DMoN at least encodes something different from the adjacency (degree distribution vs edge existence), so $B$ has a non-trivial signal --- even if it's the wrong signal for pose grouping.

This explains why every sigma strategy failed in the same way: the spatial kernel $P$ and the spatial kNN adjacency $A$ are both functions of the same 2D positions. Changing sigma just changes how closely $R$ approximates $A$ --- too broad and $R$ is weaker than $A$ (trivially exceeded), too tight and $R$ matches $A$ (nothing left). There is no sigma where the difference is both non-trivial and informative.


\subsubsection{The fix: decouple the graphs}

The adjacency and the null model need to encode \emph{different information} so that their difference is meaningful. The fix is to build the adjacency for modularity from GAT \emph{embedding similarity} rather than spatial proximity, while keeping the spatial/skeletal null model unchanged.

The GAT still runs message passing on the original spatial kNN graph --- that doesn't change. But for the modularity computation, a new adjacency is built from kNN in embedding space:
\begin{align*}
H &= \text{GAT}(A_{\text{spatial}}, X) && \text{(message passing on spatial graph)} \\
A_{\text{feature}} &= \text{kNN}(H) && \text{(build new graph from embeddings)} \\
B_{\text{pose}} &= A_{\text{feature}} - R_{\text{norm}} && \text{(modularity matrix)}
\end{align*}

Now $B_{\text{pose}} = A_{\text{feature}} - R_{\text{spatial}}$ is asking a meaningful question: ``which keypoints are connected by learned feature similarity \emph{beyond} what spatial proximity and anatomy predict?'' A left shoulder and left elbow from different people might be spatially close (high $R_{\text{norm}}$) but have dissimilar embeddings (low $A_{\text{feature}}$), giving a negative $B_{\text{pose}}$ entry --- evidence they should be in different clusters. Two keypoints from the same person that are far apart but have similar embeddings give a positive $B_{\text{pose}}$ entry --- the modularity objective rewards grouping them.

The feature adjacency is detached from the gradient --- it is a fixed graph structure for each forward pass, built from the current embeddings but not differentiated through. This prevents the modularity loss from gaming the adjacency the way it gamed sigma.


\subsubsection{The second fix: entropy-based type exclusivity}

The original type exclusivity penalty $\|\text{ReLU}(M^\top S - 1)\|_F^2$ has a problem with softmax assignments. With softmax, each keypoint distributes mass across all clusters. If there are 5 left elbows and 3 person clusters, each cluster might get $5/3 \approx 1.67$ soft mass for the left-elbow type --- but that is the expected behaviour of softmax, not a real violation. The penalty fires constantly and becomes noisy gradient signal.

The fix replaces the absolute-mass threshold with an entropy minimisation. For each cluster $k$ and joint type $t$, normalise the soft assignments of type-$t$ keypoints to cluster $k$ into a probability distribution, then minimise its entropy:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{type}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|} \sum_{(k,t) \in \mathcal{P}} H\left(\frac{S[\text{type}_t, k]}{\sum S[\text{type}_t, k]}\right)
\]

This says ``for each cluster-type pair, the assignment should be dominated by one keypoint, not spread across many.'' Low entropy means one keypoint has most of the mass (correct --- one person has one left elbow). High entropy means the mass is spread evenly (wrong --- multiple left elbows competing for the same person slot). This is a softer and more gradient-friendly signal than the ReLU threshold.


\subsubsection{Implementation}

Both fixes are implemented in \texttt{sa\_dmon\_v2.py} with config flags for independent ablation:
\begin{compactitem}
  \item \texttt{use\_feature\_adjacency}: if true, build adjacency from kNN in embedding space for modularity; if false, use the spatial graph (original behaviour)
  \item \texttt{use\_entropy\_type\_loss}: if true, use entropy-based type exclusivity; if false, use the original ReLU penalty
  \item \texttt{feature\_knn\_k}: number of neighbours for the feature adjacency (default 8)
\end{compactitem}

Because the feature adjacency now encodes different information from the spatial null, $\lambda_{\text{spectral}}$ is set to 1.0 for the feature adjacency configs. The original SA-DMoN needed 0.1 because the spectral signal was wrong and had to be suppressed --- with decoupled graphs, the spectral signal should be meaningful and can be given full weight.

\subsubsection{Conclusion: the DMoN head architecture is the bottleneck}

Six v1 experiments and four v2 experiments all converge to the same 0.77--0.81 PGA range regardless of:
\begin{compactitem}
  \item What the spectral loss measures (degree null, spatial null, feature null, or nothing)
  \item How sigma is handled (learned, clamped, fixed, or removed entirely)
  \item What type of adjacency is used (spatial kNN or embedding kNN)
  \item How type exclusivity is formulated (ReLU threshold or entropy minimisation)
\end{compactitem}

The consistent $\sim$8 point gap between the head and kNN on the same embeddings is not a loss problem --- it is an \textbf{assignment mechanism problem}. The DMoN head computes soft assignments via a single-pass dot product between encoded node features and learned cluster centres, followed by softmax. This is fundamentally weaker than k-means, which iteratively refines centroids to minimise within-cluster variance. The single-pass dot product has no iterative refinement, no convergence guarantee, and distributes mass across all clusters via softmax even when the correct assignment is unambiguous.

This is the architectural insight that motivated the pivot to SCOT. SCOT replaces the soft assignment mechanism with Sinkhorn optimal transport, which is iterative (10 iterations), globally optimal (within the entropy-regularised space), and incorporates hard type constraints that the DMoN softmax cannot express. The 0.923 PGA that SCOT achieves --- compared to the 0.805 ceiling of DMoN --- is not because SCOT has a better loss function. It is because SCOT has a better assignment mechanism.

\subsection{SCOT --- Skeleton-Constrained Optimal Transport}

\subsubsection{Why pivot from DMoN}

The SA-DMoN experiments exhaustively showed that modularity-based graph clustering is the wrong formulation for pose grouping. The kNN graph is built on spatial proximity, but the ``communities'' (people) are defined by identity --- two joints from different people are kNN neighbours whenever people are close together, which is the norm not the exception. No null model fixes this because modularity on a spatial kNN graph is asking the wrong question.

The deeper insight from the SA-DMoN experiments is that type exclusivity --- the constraint that a person has at most one keypoint of each type --- was the one consistently useful modification. But in DMoN it was a soft penalty fighting against a clustering objective that didn't naturally want it. The question became: is there a formulation where type exclusivity is a hard architectural constraint rather than a soft loss term?

\subsubsection{The formulation}

Pose grouping is not a clustering problem --- it is a \textbf{structured assignment problem}. Clustering says ``put similar things together.'' Assignment says ``fill specific slots with specific items under constraints.'' Every person is a template with 17 typed slots (one per COCO joint type), and each detected keypoint can only fill a slot matching its type.

Given $N$ detected keypoints and $K$ people, define $K \times 17$ target slots. The transport plan $T \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times (K \cdot 17)}$ assigns keypoints to slots.

The cost matrix encodes the skeleton awareness:
\[
C_{i,(k,t)} = \begin{cases}
\|h_i - p_k\|^2 & \text{if } \mathrm{type}(i) = t \\
+\infty & \text{if } \mathrm{type}(i) \neq t
\end{cases}
\]

where $h_i$ is the encoded GAT embedding for keypoint $i$ and $p_k$ is a learned prototype for person $k$. The infinity costs mean that the Sinkhorn solver \textbf{cannot} assign a left elbow to a right knee slot. It is not penalised --- it is structurally impossible.

The transport plan is computed via differentiable Sinkhorn-Knopp~\cite{cuturi2013sinkhorn} iterations in log domain:
\begin{compactitem}
  \item Source marginal: each keypoint assigned to exactly one slot (row sum = 1)
  \item Target marginal: each person-type slot gets at most one keypoint (column sum $\leq 1$)
  \item A slack column absorbs unmatched slots (handles missing/occluded joints)
\end{compactitem}

Person-level logits are obtained by summing the transport plan over type slots per person: $\mathrm{logits}_{i,k} = \sum_t T_{i,(k \cdot 17 + t)}$. Training uses Hungarian-matched cross entropy on these logits --- the same supervised signal as slot attention.

\subsubsection{Why this is novel}

Standard optimal transport for assignment (e.g.\ OTA~\cite{ge2021ota} for object detection) uses flat targets --- anchors assigned to objects with no typed structure. SCOT has a two-level structure: person level and keypoint-type level. The target is not just $K$ persons but $K \times 17$ typed slots. This hierarchical typed target is specific to the pose problem.

The same-type exclusivity is now impossible to violate by construction rather than discouraged by a penalty. Two left elbows competing for the same person will be forced into different person slots because there is only one left-elbow slot per person. This is fundamentally different from DMoN's $\|\mathrm{ReLU}(M^\top S - 1)\|_F^2$ which can only discourage violations after they occur.


\subsubsection{Implementation}

The SCOT head (\texttt{ot\_head.py}) follows the same pattern as other heads:

\begin{compactitem}
  \item Node encoder: Linear($D$, $H$) $\to$ SELU --- maps GAT embeddings to assignment space
  \item Person prototype pool: $K_{\max}$ learned vectors, first $K$ selected per scene
  \item Cost matrix: squared distance between encoded nodes and prototypes, with $10^6$ cost for type mismatches
  \item Sinkhorn iterations (10, temperature $\tau = 0.1$) in log domain for numerical stability
  \item Slack column with capacity $\max(N - K \cdot 17, 1)$ for unmatched slots
\end{compactitem}

Config:
\begin{lstlisting}[style=pythonstyle]
scot:
  hidden_dim: 256
  k_max: 10
  sinkhorn_iters: 10
  sinkhorn_tau: 0.1
\end{lstlisting}

No spectral loss, no null model, no sigma parameter. The only loss is contrastive (GAT) + Hungarian-matched cross entropy (head).

\subsubsection{Analysis}

\textbf{1. SCOT is the first head to beat kNN on synthetic data.} At 0.923 vs 0.886, the 3.7 point margin is substantial. The hard type constraint via the OT cost matrix is what makes the difference --- every previous head used either no type constraint (slot attention, graph partitioning) or a soft penalty (DMoN/SA-DMoN type loss). Making type exclusivity structurally impossible to violate rather than penalised is the key architectural insight.

\textbf{2. The SCOT-trained GAT barely degrades.} kNN on SCOT GAT (0.886) vs contrastive-only GAT (0.901) is a 1.5 point gap --- the same as SA-DMoN's best and much less than vanilla DMoN (2.0 points). The SCOT loss cooperates with contrastive learning because both objectives want the same thing: same-person embeddings close, different-person embeddings far. DMoN's spectral loss actively fought the contrastive objective.

\textbf{3. Training is dramatically cleaner than DMoN.} Head loss drops smoothly from 1.17 to 0.02 over 100 epochs. No oscillation, no fighting between objectives, no sigma divergence. The best checkpoint is at epoch 30 (val PGA 0.905) --- the model learns fast because the type constraint removes a large portion of the search space that other heads waste capacity exploring.

\textbf{4. On COCO, SCOT (0.862) is the best head result by far but does not beat kNN (0.877).} Previous best head on COCO was SA-DMoN at 0.817. SCOT beats that by 4.5 points. The gap to kNN on COCO (1.5 points) is the smallest of any head tested.

\textbf{5. The COCO per-joint pattern reveals where transfer fails.} SCOT beats kNN on torso joints (shoulders, hips) where the type constraint provides the most value --- these are the joints most likely to have same-type neighbours from other people. But SCOT loses on head joints (eyes 0.797--0.818 vs 0.885--0.898, ears 0.733--0.766 vs 0.848--0.873). Face keypoints cluster tightly in 2D and all face types are distinct already, so the type constraint adds nothing there. The prototype-based cost function struggles when embeddings are noisy for these small, densely packed joints on real images.

\textbf{6. The sim-to-real gap is larger for SCOT ($-6.1$) than kNN ($-2.2$).} SCOT's synthetic advantage (0.923 vs 0.886) does not fully transfer --- the head's learned cost function and Sinkhorn transport plan are more sensitive to distributional shift than simple k-means. This suggests the next step is either fine-tuning on a small amount of real data or making the cost function more robust to embedding noise.

\subsubsection{Experiment 13 --- Residual SCOT (spatial prior + learned correction)}

\paragraph{Motivation.}

SCOT beats kNN on synthetic data but loses on COCO. One hypothesis for why: kNN is a local, greedy method --- each keypoint independently picks its nearest cluster centre. On easy scenes where people are well-separated, greedy is optimal. OT is a global method --- it finds the best overall assignment considering all keypoints simultaneously. That global reasoning helps on hard scenes (overlapping people) but on easy scenes it introduces noise from the Sinkhorn entropy regularisation, which smears assignments that should be sharp.

The idea: decompose the cost matrix into a spatial prior and a learned correction:
\[
C_{\text{final}} = C_{\text{spatial}} + \lambda \cdot C_{\text{learned}}
\]

$C_{\text{spatial}}$ is the squared distance from each keypoint to $K$ spatial centroids computed via k-means on 2D positions (detached, not learned). This encodes geometric proximity --- equivalent to what kNN uses. $C_{\text{learned}}$ is the squared distance from encoded GAT embeddings to learned prototypes --- the same cost as vanilla SCOT.

On easy scenes, $C_{\text{spatial}}$ already has the right answer and the learned residual barely matters. On hard scenes, $C_{\text{spatial}}$ is ambiguous and $C_{\text{learned}}$ provides the discriminative correction. The spatial prior anchors easy cases, the learned cost handles hard cases.

This was implemented in a separate file (\texttt{ot\_head\_residual.py}) to preserve the original SCOT head for ablation. The $\lambda$ parameter was set to 0.2, meaning the spatial cost contributes 80\% and the learned cost 20\%.

\subsubsection{Experiment 14 --- Adaptive SCOT (scene-predicted temperature)}

\paragraph{Motivation.}

The Sinkhorn temperature $\tau$ controls how ``soft'' the transport plan is. High $\tau$ gives smooth, spread-out assignments (global OT reasoning). Low $\tau$ approaches hard, greedy assignment (kNN-like). Vanilla SCOT fixes $\tau = 0.1$, which may not be optimal for all scenes --- easy scenes with well-separated people might benefit from lower $\tau$ (sharper), while hard scenes with overlapping people might benefit from higher $\tau$ (more global).

The adaptive variant predicts $\tau$ from the scene:
\[
\tau = \sigma(\text{MLP}(\text{graph\_summary})) \cdot (\tau_{\max} - \tau_{\min}) + \tau_{\min}
\]

where the graph summary is computed from cost matrix statistics (mean, standard deviation, row-wise entropy) and mean-pooled GAT embeddings. The model learns when to trust local assignments and when to invoke global reasoning. Implemented in a separate file (\texttt{ot\_head\_adaptive.py}) with $\tau_{\min} = 0.01$, $\tau_{\max} = 0.5$.

\subsubsection{Experiment 15 --- Unbalanced SCOT (K-free assignment)}

\paragraph{Motivation.}

All previous SCOT variants require the number of people $K$ as input. In practice $K$ is unknown at inference. Unbalanced optimal transport addresses this by relaxing the target marginal constraint via a KL divergence penalty. Instead of requiring every person-type slot to be filled, unused slots naturally receive near-zero assignment mass.

The formulation uses $K_{\max} = 20$ person templates (well above the expected maximum). The unbalanced Sinkhorn algorithm modifies the column normalisation step:
\[
\text{standard: } \log v = \log c - \text{logsumexp}(\ldots)
\]
\[
\text{unbalanced: } \log v = \frac{\rho}{\rho + \tau} \cdot (\log c - \text{logsumexp}(\ldots))
\]

The scaling factor $\rho / (\rho + \tau) < 1$ dampens column normalisation, allowing target slots to deviate from their marginal. As $\rho \to \infty$, standard Sinkhorn is recovered. The number of people emerges from the solution: person $k$ exists if $\sum_i T_{i,(k,\cdot)} > \text{threshold}$.

Implemented in \texttt{ot\_head\_unbalanced.py} with $\rho = 1.0$, $K_{\max} = 20$, 20 Sinkhorn iterations, person threshold 0.5. During training, ground truth $K$ is still provided so the loss operates on the correct number of logit columns.

\subsubsection{Experiment 16 --- Dustbin SCOT (K-free via explicit rejection)}

\paragraph{Motivation.}

The unbalanced approach relaxed target marginals via KL penalty, which made assignments noisy. This is the approach used by DETR for object detection --- a learned ``no object'' class that absorbs unused queries. The key difference from unbalanced OT: the dustbin is an explicit competing option with a learned cost, not a relaxation of the marginal constraints.

Implemented in \texttt{ot\_head\_dustbin.py} with $K_{\max} = 20$, learned dustbin cost initialised at 1.0, person threshold 0.5. During training, GT $K$ is provided for the logit columns (same as unbalanced).

\subsection{COP-Kmeans --- Type-Constrained K-Means}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

SCOT demonstrated that the type constraint is the key contribution --- hard enforcement that no two keypoints of the same type can be assigned to the same person. But SCOT wraps this constraint inside a learned optimal transport formulation with person prototypes, Sinkhorn iterations, and supervised training. What if the constraint is applied directly to the kNN baseline instead?

COP-Kmeans~\cite{wagstaff2001copkmeans} (Wagstaff et al.\ 2001) extends standard k-means with \textbf{must-not-link constraints}: pairs of points that are forbidden from being in the same cluster. For pose grouping, the constraints are simple: any two keypoints of the same type cannot be in the same cluster. A left elbow and another left elbow must be in different person clusters.

This is the minimal possible intervention on the kNN baseline --- the same algorithm, the same embeddings, the same $K$, with one additional constraint. No training required. If the type constraint is the reason SCOT works, COP-Kmeans should capture most of the benefit without any of the complexity.


\subsubsection{How it works}

The algorithm is identical to standard k-means with one change in the assignment step:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Initialise centroids with k-means++
  \item \textbf{Constrained assignment:} sort keypoints by distance to nearest centroid (most confident first). For each keypoint, assign to the nearest centroid whose cluster does not already contain a keypoint of the same type. If the nearest centroid violates the constraint, try the next nearest, and so on.
  \item \textbf{Update:} recompute centroids as cluster means
  \item Repeat until convergence (10 random restarts, best kept)
\end{enumerate}

The confidence-first ordering ensures that ambiguous keypoints (far from all centroids) are resolved after the easy ones are settled. No training is required --- COP-Kmeans runs on the existing contrastive GAT embeddings at inference time using the same checkpoint as the kNN baseline.

\subsection{Type-Constrained Agglomerative Clustering}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

COP-Kmeans showed that type constraints on k-means produce strong results. A natural question: does the same constraint help with a different base algorithm? Agglomerative (bottom-up) clustering starts with each keypoint as its own cluster and iteratively merges the two closest clusters. Adding the type constraint means: never merge two clusters that would create duplicate joint types.

This has a potential advantage over COP-Kmeans: it doesn't need $K$ at inference. Standard agglomerative clustering stops at a target $K$, but type-constrained agglomerative could stop when all remaining merges would violate constraints --- $K$ emerges from the data. For this evaluation, GT $K$ is provided for fair comparison, but the method could work without it.


\subsubsection{How it works}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Start with $N$ clusters (one per keypoint)
  \item Find the closest pair of clusters (average linkage --- mean pairwise distance between members)
  \item If merging would create duplicate joint types in the merged cluster, skip this pair and try the next closest
  \item Merge the closest valid pair
  \item Repeat until $K$ clusters remain
\end{enumerate}

No training required --- runs on the same contrastive GAT embeddings as kNN and COP-Kmeans.

If it merges the wrong pair early (because they are the closest valid pair), every subsequent merge is conditioned on that error. K-means can recover from bad assignments by recomputing centroids on the next iteration. This is why COP-Kmeans (0.913/0.910) dramatically outperforms agglomerative (0.837/0.887) despite using the same type constraint.

\textbf{5. Conclusion: the base algorithm matters as much as the constraint.} The type constraint helps both methods on COCO, confirming it is a genuinely useful inductive bias for sim-to-real transfer. But COP-Kmeans is the right vehicle for it --- k-means' iterative centroid refinement is a better assignment mechanism than agglomerative's greedy bottom-up merging, and the constraint integrates cleanly into the assignment step without the irrevocable-decision problem.

\subsection{Skeleton-Aware Spectral Clustering}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

The third type-constrained variant: apply the constraint to spectral clustering. Build an affinity matrix from GAT embedding similarity (RBF kernel on cosine distance), then zero out affinities between same-type keypoints. Spectral clustering computes eigenvectors of the modified graph Laplacian and runs k-means in the spectral embedding space. The type constraint lives in the graph structure --- same-type keypoints are disconnected in the affinity graph, so the Laplacian naturally separates them.

This is more mathematically principled than COP-Kmeans --- the constraint modifies the graph that spectral clustering reasons about, rather than being a greedy check during assignment. No training required.

\subsection{SA-GAT --- Skeleton-Aware Graph Attention Network}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

All experiments so far modified the \emph{grouping method} on top of the GAT embeddings. COP-Kmeans showed that a simple type constraint on k-means beats every learned head. But the embeddings themselves are still produced by a standard GAT that treats all edges identically --- a left-shoulder-to-left-elbow edge gets the same attention function as a left-shoulder-to-right-ankle edge. These edges carry fundamentally different information for grouping.

The idea: make the GAT backbone itself skeleton-aware so that the embeddings are inherently better for grouping, regardless of which downstream method is used. This is an independent contribution from the grouping constraint --- SA-GAT improves the embeddings, COP-Kmeans improves the assignment. Together they should compose.


\subsubsection{Three modifications}

Each modification is independently configurable for ablation.

\paragraph{Modification 1: Type-Pair Attention Bias.}

Categorise every edge in the graph based on the joint types at each end:

\begin{compactitem}
  \item \texttt{SAME\_TYPE} --- both keypoints are the same type (e.g.\ two left elbows). These \emph{must} belong to different people. The attention should learn inter-person discrimination.
  \item \texttt{SKELETAL\_NEIGHBOR} --- directly connected on the COCO skeleton (e.g.\ shoulder--elbow). Strongest cue for same-person grouping --- anatomically connected joints from the same person have consistent spatial relationships.
  \item \texttt{SAME\_LIMB} --- on the same limb but not directly adjacent (e.g.\ shoulder--wrist). Medium-range structural information.
  \item \texttt{CROSS\_BODY} --- unrelated body parts (e.g.\ left wrist--right ankle). Weak or no structural prior.
\end{compactitem}

Each edge gets a one-hot category encoding $[4]$ which is passed through an MLP and added as an attention bias via GATv2Conv's \texttt{edge\_dim} mechanism. This lets the attention function weight different categories of interactions differently without replacing the core GATv2Conv implementation.

\paragraph{Modification 2: Skeleton-Relative Position Encoding.}

Standard GAT uses no explicit spatial information in attention --- it is purely feature-based. Three edge features are computed and encoded as an additional attention bias:

\begin{compactitem}
  \item Spatial distance $\|pos_i - pos_j\|$ between the two keypoints
  \item Skeleton hop distance between their types (normalised), from the COCO skeleton graph
  \item Same-type indicator (1 if same type, 0 otherwise)
\end{compactitem}

These are concatenated with the category one-hot and passed through the same edge encoder MLP. The skeleton hop distance tells the attention how anatomically related two keypoint types are, regardless of their spatial proximity.

\paragraph{Modification 3: Same-Type Repulsion Heads.}

In a multi-head attention setup, dedicate one or more heads exclusively to same-type neighbor interactions. Standard heads attend to all neighbors. The repulsion head only receives edges connecting same-type keypoints --- all other edges are masked out.

This forces the repulsion head to explicitly process the inter-person signal: ``I have two left elbows --- how should their embeddings differ?'' The contrastive loss then pushes the repulsion head to produce discriminative features for distinguishing same-type keypoints belonging to different people.

Implementation: the SA-GAT layer splits into a standard GATv2Conv (3 heads, all edges) and a repulsion GATv2Conv (1 head, same-type edges only). Their outputs are concatenated, giving the same total dimension as 4 standard heads.


\subsubsection{Implementation}

SA-GAT (\texttt{sa\_gat.py}) is a drop-in replacement for the standard \texttt{GATEmbedding} --- same forward signature, same output shape $[N, 128]$. All downstream grouping methods work without changes.

The key design decision: build on top of PyG's optimised GATv2Conv rather than reimplementing attention from scratch. The skeleton-aware modifications are injected as edge features (\texttt{edge\_attr}) that GATv2Conv adds as attention biases. This preserves numerical stability and performance while adding the skeleton-aware signal. An initial implementation with custom scatter-based attention failed catastrophically (PGA 0.60) due to numerical instability in the manual softmax --- switching to GATv2Conv with edge features fixed this immediately.

Config flags for ablation:
\begin{compactitem}
  \item \texttt{use\_type\_pair\_attention}: category-specific attention bias
  \item \texttt{use\_position\_encoding}: spatial + skeleton hop encoding
  \item \texttt{use\_repulsion\_heads}: dedicated same-type heads
  \item \texttt{n\_repulsion\_heads}: how many heads reserved (default 1 of 4)
\end{compactitem}

\textbf{4. The full combination is best.} SA-GAT full (0.925 / 0.915) outperforms every individual modification. The three modifications are complementary: type-pair attention provides category-specific parameters, position encoding provides geometric context, and repulsion heads provide dedicated inter-person processing. \subsection{K Estimation --- Removing the Ground Truth K Dependency}

\subsubsection{The problem}

Every method tested in this project receives the ground truth number of people $K$ as input. kNN uses it as \texttt{n\_clusters}, COP-Kmeans uses it identically, SCOT constructs $K \times 17$ typed slots. In a real production system $K$ is unknown --- the system receives detected keypoints and must determine both how many people are present and which keypoints belong to which person.

This is standard practice in bottom-up pose grouping research --- most papers evaluate with GT keypoint locations and GT person count to isolate the grouping problem from detection. But solving K estimation would make the system complete and deployable.


\subsubsection{Attempt 1: Eigenvalue gap}

The classical spectral approach: build an affinity matrix from embedding similarity (RBF kernel), compute the graph Laplacian $L = D - W$, and estimate $K$ from the largest gap between consecutive eigenvalues. The number of near-zero eigenvalues equals the number of connected components (people).

\paragraph{Results.}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule

\midrule
Exact match & 26\% & 27\% & 18\% \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Eigenvalue gap K estimation accuracy.}
\end{table}

Per GT K breakdown (SA-GAT, synthetic):
\begin{compactitem}
  \item K=2: 92\% exact --- near perfect
  \item K=3: 12\% exact --- mostly predicted K=2
  \item K=4: 0\% exact --- all predicted K=2 or K=3
  \item K=5: 0\% exact --- same failure
\end{compactitem}

The eigengap is massively biased toward K=2. The contrastive loss produces embeddings where the first spectral gap (between eigenvalue 1 and 2) is always the largest --- the strongest signal is ``there are multiple people'' (a binary split). The finer structure (3, 4, 5 people) produces smaller gaps that the simple argmax misses.

Grouping PGA with predicted K drops catastrophically: 0.910 $\to$ 0.673 on synthetic ($-$23.7 points). The eigengap approach is not viable.


\subsubsection{Attempt 2: Learned K estimation head}

A small MLP that predicts $K$ from pooled GAT embeddings and the keypoint count:
\[
K_{\text{pred}} = \text{MLP}(\text{mean\_pool}(H) \,\|\, \text{max\_pool}(H) \,\|\, N_{\text{keypoints}} / 17)
\]

The $N_{\text{keypoints}} / 17$ feature is a strong prior --- with 17 COCO joints per person, 85 keypoints almost certainly means 5 people. The MLP learns to refine this estimate using the embedding features (which encode how many distinct clusters exist). Trained jointly with the SA-GAT via L1 loss on the predicted K.

\paragraph{Results --- Synthetic.}

\paragraph{The embedding quality problem.}

The K head achieved perfect K estimation on synthetic data but the grouping PGA was only 0.677 --- dramatically below the SA-GAT without K head (0.910). On COCO the same problem: kNN with GT K scored 0.793 (vs 0.882 for SA-GAT without K head). The K head loss corrupted the embeddings.


\subsubsection{Attempt 3: Frozen backbone K head}

The fix: freeze the SA-GAT weights and train only the K head on pre-computed embeddings. The SA-GAT checkpoint (0.910/0.882) is loaded and frozen --- the K head learns to read $K$ from embeddings that were already optimised for separation, without corrupting them.

Implemented in \texttt{train\_k\_head\_frozen.py}: loads a GAT checkpoint, freezes all parameters, creates a fresh K head, and trains with L1 loss for 50 epochs on the virtual training set.

K estimation: \textbf{100/100 exact match} (identical to joint training). Crucially, the embeddings are preserved: kNN PGA 0.910 and COP-Kmeans PGA 0.925, both identical to the SA-GAT without any K head. Grouping with predicted K matches grouping with GT K exactly ($+$0.000).

\subsection{Training Infrastructure --- GUID-based Output Management}

\subsubsection{The problem}

During SCOT re-training, I discovered that re-running \texttt{trainer.py} with the same config overwrites the previous \texttt{best.pt} checkpoint. The original SCOT run achieved 0.923 synthetic PGA; after accidentally re-training with the same config (but only 50 epochs instead of 100), the checkpoint was overwritten with a 0.917 result. The original model was permanently lost.

This is a fundamental reproducibility problem. The old directory structure was:
\begin{verbatim}
outputs/checkpoints/{config_save_dir}/
    best.pt        # overwritten on every run
    final.pt
    history.json
\end{verbatim}

There was a one-to-one mapping between config and output directory, so any re-run --- whether intentional (parameter sweep) or accidental --- destroyed the previous result. There was also no record of which exact config produced a given checkpoint, making it impossible to reproduce a run after the fact.

\subsubsection{Solution: GUID-based run directories}

Every training run now creates a unique subdirectory under \texttt{outputs/\{config\_name\}/}:
\begin{verbatim}
outputs/
  train_scot_no_depth/
    a1b2c3d4/             # 8-char GUID
      best.pt
      final.pt
      history.json
      config.yaml          # full copy of the config used
      eval_results.json    # saved by evaluator
    e5f6g7h8/             # another run with same or different params
      ...
    latest -> a1b2c3d4    # symlink to most recent run
\end{verbatim}

Key design decisions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The config name is derived from the YAML filename (e.g.\ \texttt{configs/train\_scot\_no\_depth.yaml} $\to$ \texttt{train\_scot\_no\_depth}). A \texttt{--name} override is available for re-running a saved \texttt{config.yaml} from inside a GUID directory, which would otherwise create a folder called ``config''.
    \item The full config YAML is copied into each run directory, so any result can be reproduced by pointing the trainer at that file: \texttt{python trainer.py --config outputs/.../config.yaml --name train\_scot\_no\_depth}.
    \item A \texttt{latest} symlink is updated programmatically after each run, so quick evaluations can use \texttt{outputs/train\_scot\_no\_depth/latest/best.pt} without knowing the GUID.
    \item Evaluation results are saved next to the checkpoint rather than to a global \texttt{outputs/eval\_results.json} that was also being overwritten.
\end{itemize}

Previous frozen results from all experiments up to this point were preserved \texttt{outputs/frozen\_checkpoints/}.


\subsection{End-to-End Evaluation --- HigherHRNet~\cite{cheng2020higherhrnet} Detection + SA-GAT Grouping}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

All experiments up to this point assume ground truth keypoint locations. This is useful for isolating the grouping contribution, but does not reflect a real deployment scenario where keypoints must first be detected. To compare against published methods like HigherHRNet, DEKR~\cite{geng2021dekr}, and ED-Pose~\cite{yang2023edpose}, I need to evaluate on the same terms they use: raw images in, per-person poses out.

The obvious approach would be to isolate each method's grouping stage --- feed GT keypoints into their grouping logic and compare directly. However, this would require deep surgery into each model's codebase to bypass detection and inject GT keypoints. These models tightly couple detection and grouping (e.g.\ HigherHRNet jointly predicts heatmaps and associative embedding tags in a single forward pass), making clean separation impractical without risking subtle bugs that would invalidate the comparison.

Instead, I chose the end-to-end approach: let every method run its complete pipeline on the same images, then compare the final per-person keypoint assignments. This means each method uses its own detector, so differences in detection quality are conflated with differences in grouping quality. The caveat is clear: \emph{a better detector gives the grouper easier inputs}. To make this concrete, I also pair SA-GAT with the same HigherHRNet detector, so the comparison between HigherHRNet's AE grouping and SA-GAT's COP-Kmeans grouping uses identical detections --- isolating the grouping contribution without any model surgery.

This also enables direct comparison against HigherHRNet's own associative embedding~\cite{newell2017associative} (AE) grouping, which is trained end-to-end on COCO --- a much stronger training signal than the ~400 synthetic images.

\subsubsection{Setup}

I integrated \texttt{simple-HigherHRNet}, a clean PyTorch wrapper around the official HigherHRNet weights, into the codebase as a vendor dependency. The pretrained model used is \textbf{HigherHRNet-W32 at 512px resolution} --- the smallest available variant (the W32-640 and W48-640 weights were no longer available from the official Google Drive links).

The end-to-end pipeline works as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Detection:} HigherHRNet processes each COCO image and produces per-joint-type heatmaps. Its internal NMS extracts keypoint candidates and its associative embedding tags group them into people.
    \item \textbf{Keypoint matching:} Detected keypoints are matched to ground truth keypoints by joint type and spatial proximity (Hungarian assignment, 10px threshold). This gives each matched detection a GT person label for evaluation.
    \item \textbf{SA-GAT grouping:} The matched detections (now unassigned --- HigherHRNet's grouping is discarded) are fed into the SA-GAT preprocessor to build a kNN graph. SA-GAT produces embeddings, and COP-Kmeans groups them.
    \item \textbf{PGA evaluation:} Three groupings are compared against GT person labels: HigherHRNet's own AE grouping, SA-GAT + kNN, and SA-GAT + COP-Kmeans.
\end{enumerate}

Evaluated on 2,166 COCO val images (images with $\geq 1$ person having $\geq 3$ visible keypoints).

HigherHRNet-W32-512 detects more keypoints than exist in the ground truth (90k vs 67k), many of which are false positives or duplicates. 79.3\% of GT keypoints were successfully matched to a detection within 10 pixels.

\subsubsection{Analysis}

\textbf{1. HigherHRNet's AE grouping is very strong at 0.985.} This is expected: HigherHRNet was trained end-to-end on COCO with associative embedding loss, so its tag embeddings are perfectly calibrated for its own detections. The detector and grouper were jointly optimised on hundreds of thousands of real images. This is the advantage of end-to-end training on the target domain.

\textbf{2. SA-GAT + COP-Kmeans at 0.908 is remarkably close given the training data gap.} SA-GAT was trained on just 400 synthetic images with perfect keypoint positions, yet it achieves 0.908 PGA on real detected keypoints from a completely different detector. The drop from GT keypoints (0.915) to detected keypoints (0.908) is only 0.7 points, showing that SA-GAT's contrastive embeddings are robust to detection noise --- position jitter, false positives being filtered out, and missing keypoints.

\textbf{3. The 7.7-point gap to HigherHRNet is a training data issue, not an architectural limitation.} HigherHRNet was trained on COCO (118k training images, real-world diversity, end-to-end backpropagation through detector and grouper). SA-GAT was trained on 400 procedurally generated scenes with no occlusion modelling, no real backgrounds, and no detection noise. The fact that SA-GAT closes to within 8 points of a COCO-trained model using two orders of magnitude less training data suggests the architecture has significant headroom. Fine-tuning on COCO or training on a larger synthetic dataset would likely close this gap substantially.

\textbf{4. The W32-512 model is the weakest HigherHRNet variant.} The W48-640 model would have higher detection recall and likely better AE grouping. However, this also means SA-GAT would receive higher-quality keypoint candidates, potentially improving its PGA as well. The relative comparison is what matters for the thesis.

\textbf{5. COP-Kmeans consistently outperforms kNN in the end-to-end setting.} The 3.2-point gap (0.908 vs 0.876) is slightly larger than in the GT keypoint setting (0.915 vs 0.882, gap 3.3 points). Type constraints remain valuable when keypoints come from a real detector.


\subsection{Full Autonomous Pipeline --- SA-GAT + K-Head + SCOT End-to-End}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

The previous end-to-end evaluation used GT person count (K) for the grouping methods. In a real deployment there is no oracle --- the system must estimate K from the scene. This section evaluates the complete PEng pipeline with no ground truth dependencies: HigherHRNet detects keypoints, SA-GAT embeds them, the K-head predicts how many people are present, and SCOT (or COP-Kmeans) groups the keypoints into that many people.

This is also the first time SCOT has been evaluated end-to-end with detected keypoints, which tests whether the learned transport prototypes generalise beyond the synthetic training distribution.

\subsubsection{Setup}

I trained a new K estimation head on frozen SA-GAT+SCOT embeddings (the K-head from the earlier section was trained on SA-GAT-only embeddings, which occupy a different embedding space). The new K-head achieved 100\% exact match on synthetic validation, consistent with the earlier result --- the frozen backbone approach works regardless of which SA-GAT variant provides the embeddings.

The pipeline for each COCO image:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item HigherHRNet-W32-512 detects keypoints (same detector as previous section).
    \item Detected keypoints are matched to GT by type and proximity (10px threshold).
    \item SA-GAT (from the SA-GAT+SCOT checkpoint) embeds the matched keypoints.
    \item The K-head predicts the number of people from the embeddings.
    \item SCOT groups keypoints using the predicted K; COP-Kmeans also groups using predicted K for comparison.
\end{enumerate}

Evaluated on 2,166 COCO val images (same set as previous section).

\subsubsection{Analysis}

\textbf{1. The SCOT transfer gap persists in the end-to-end setting.} With GT K, SCOT scores 0.854 vs COP-Kmeans at 0.908 --- a 5.4-point gap, consistent with the GT keypoint results (0.863 vs 0.915). The learned prototypes and Sinkhorn transport plan, trained on 400 synthetic scenes, do not capture the spatial diversity of real COCO images. This is the primary weakness of the SCOT approach in its current form and the clearest target for improvement: fine-tuning on COCO data or training on a more diverse synthetic dataset should close this gap.

\textbf{2. K estimation adds a further 5.4-point drop for SCOT (0.854 $\to$ 0.800).} SCOT is more sensitive to K errors than COP-Kmeans because the Sinkhorn transport plan creates exactly $K \times 17$ typed slots. If K is wrong, the slot structure is fundamentally misaligned --- too few slots force keypoints into incorrect people, and too many slots leave empty clusters that fragment correct groupings. COP-Kmeans handles K errors more gracefully because k-means centroids can absorb extra or missing clusters with less structural damage.

\textbf{3. COP-Kmeans outperforms SCOT across every evaluation condition.} With GT keypoints, with detected keypoints, with GT K, and with predicted K. The gap ranges from 1.6 to 5.4 points. For the MEng contribution this is a strong result --- COP-Kmeans is simpler, has no learned components beyond the SA-GAT embeddings, and transfers better. For the PEng contribution, this establishes a clear baseline that SCOT must beat after domain adaptation improvements.

\textbf{4. The fully autonomous pipeline (SA-GAT + K-head + SCOT at 0.800) is a complete system.} No ground truth of any kind is used at inference: HigherHRNet provides keypoint candidates, the K-head estimates person count, and SCOT assigns keypoints to people. While 0.800 is below the oracle-K results, it demonstrates that every component of the pipeline functions end-to-end. The three sources of error are clearly decomposed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Detection noise: $-0.9$ points (GT kps 0.863 $\to$ detected kps GT-K 0.854)
    \item K estimation error: $-5.4$ points (GT-K 0.854 $\to$ predicted-K 0.800)
    \item SCOT transfer gap: $-5.4$ points vs COP-Kmeans at each K condition
\end{itemize}
Each of these is independently addressable: better detector weights, COCO-trained K-head, and SCOT fine-tuning respectively.

\textbf{5. The 18.5-point gap to HigherHRNet (0.985 vs 0.800) decomposes cleanly.} Approximately 8 points come from the training data gap (SA-GAT trained on 400 synthetic vs HigherHRNet on 118k COCO), 5 points from SCOT's transfer weakness, and 5 points from K estimation errors. None of these are architectural limitations --- they are all addressable with more data and domain adaptation, which is exactly the PEng improvement trajectory.


\subsection{COCO Fine-Tuning --- Closing the Transfer Gap}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

The previous sections identified the synthetic-to-real transfer gap as the primary performance bottleneck. SA-GAT trained on 400 synthetic images achieved 0.915 COCO PGA with COP-Kmeans --- strong, but 7 points behind HigherHRNet's 0.985 in the end-to-end comparison. The question was whether this gap is architectural or simply a training data issue. Fine-tuning on COCO train2017 directly tests this.

\subsubsection{Setup}

I added COCO training support to the trainer --- an optional \texttt{coco\_train\_dir} and \texttt{coco\_train\_ann} in the training config, plus a \texttt{pretrained} field to load existing weights before training. When COCO paths are set, the trainer uses the CocoAdapter for training data instead of VirtualAdapter. Validation remains on synthetic data for consistent benchmarking. All changes are backwards-compatible: existing configs are unaffected since the new fields default to \texttt{None}.

Fine-tuning configuration:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Pre-trained on: SA-GAT full (400 synthetic images, 50 epochs)
    \item Fine-tuned on: COCO train2017 (56,599 images with people)
    \item Learning rate: $1 \times 10^{-4}$ (10$\times$ lower than pre-training)
    \item LR minimum: $1 \times 10^{-6}$
    \item Epochs: 20 (each epoch processes all 56k images)
    \item Head: contrastive loss only (kNN grouping, no learned head)
    \item Depth: disabled (\texttt{use\_depth: false}, MiDaS skipped)
\end{itemize}

Training took approximately 88 minutes (20 epochs $\times$ $\sim$264s per epoch). The loss decreased steadily from 0.095 to 0.060, and validation PGA improved every second epoch, reaching a best of 0.920 at epoch 20 --- still climbing, suggesting further epochs would help.

    \item 0.915 --- SA-GAT + COP-Kmeans (synthetic only, GT keypoints)
    \item 0.863 --- SA-GAT + SCOT (synthetic only, GT keypoints)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{5. The architectural contributions (type-pair attention, skeleton-relative position encoding, repulsion heads, must-not-link constraints) are genuine improvements that complement standard contrastive training.

\textbf{6. SCOT fine-tuning is the natural next step for the PEng.} The same fine-tuning approach should be applied to the SA-GAT + SCOT pipeline. The SCOT transfer gap (0.863 vs COP-Kmeans' 0.915 on synthetic-only training) may close substantially when the SCOT prototypes and Sinkhorn transport are trained on real data rather than 400 synthetic scenes.

\textbf{Correction to earlier size claim.} An initial reading of the parameter counts suggested SA-GAT is 168$\times$ smaller than HigherHRNet. This is misleading. HigherHRNet's \emph{pure grouping component} is tiny: the AE tag prediction is just 17 extra output channels on a single $1 \times 1$ convolution ($32 \times 17 + 17$ = 561 parameters). However, those 17 output channels cannot work alone --- they read from features produced by the full 28.6M-parameter HRNet backbone. The AE grouping is cheap to add but structurally dependent on the backbone.

\textbf{SA-GAT's actual advantage is modularity, not size.} At 170,560 parameters SA-GAT is roughly 300$\times$ \emph{larger} than the AE grouping head when measured in isolation. But SA-GAT operates directly on keypoint positions and types --- it has no image backbone and can be dropped onto any keypoint detector without joint training. The AE head cannot; it is only trainable end-to-end with its specific HRNet backbone. The contribution is \textbf{decoupling the grouping step from the detector}, enabling reuse of any pre-trained detector with a consistent grouping module.

SA-GAT adds only 9,952 parameters over the standard GAT (a 6.2\% increase), demonstrating that the three skeleton-aware modifications (type-pair attention, position encoding, repulsion heads) are lightweight architectural changes, not heavy additions. COP-Kmeans adds zero learned parameters. The entire MEng grouping contribution, from embedding to final person assignment, fits in 0.65\,MB and is detector-agnostic.


\subsection{State at the end of this catchup}

At this point in the project, the MEng story is clear: pose grouping is an embedding problem, and SA-GAT produces embeddings good enough that simple clustering beats every learned head tested. The best numbers are 0.968 PGA on COCO with GT keypoints and 0.955 end-to-end with HigherHRNet detection --- within 3 points of HigherHRNet's own jointly-trained AE grouping, using a modular component that can plug into any detector.

The SCOT direction is less clear-cut. On synthetic data SCOT is competitive (0.926), but on COCO it drops to 0.863 while the simpler constraint-based COP-Kmeans sits at 0.915. SCOT's learned prototypes overfit to the synthetic training distribution. The next catchup investigates this: COCO fine-tuning, frozen-backbone training, SCOT-KI (k-means initialised prototypes), and ultimately the pivot to Type-wise Hungarian Assignment (THA) which closes the gap. The MEng/PEng split also gets sharpened in the next catchup as ``clustering vs assignment'' rather than ``SA-GAT vs SCOT'' --- see the conceptual split section there.
